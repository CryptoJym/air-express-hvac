Winter Heating Troubleshooting: Why Your Furnace Isn't Working

Quick Answer: Check These 5 Things Before Calling for Repair

Before you call a technician, verify these five things on your furnace and thermostat:

  1. Thermostat Set to Heat Mode - Make sure it's not stuck on Cool or Off. The temperature setting should be above your current room temperature.
  2. Check Your Air Filter - A clogged filter restricts airflow and can prevent your furnace from starting. Replace if it's dirty (filter should be white/light colored, not gray/black).
  3. Circuit Breaker Not Tripped - Check your electrical panel. The furnace breaker should be in the ON position. If it keeps tripping, you have an electrical problem requiring service.
  4. Gas Valve is Open - Look at your gas meter and furnace connection. There should be a valve that's parallel to the gas line (open). If perpendicular, it's closed.
  5. Vents and Registers Not Blocked - Make sure return air vents are clean and supply registers aren't blocked by furniture, curtains, or vents that are shut.

If all five items check out and your furnace still won't heat, it's time to call Air Express for professional diagnostic service.

Common Winter Heating Problems and Solutions

Problem 1: Furnace Won't Turn On

Causes: Thermostat not set to heat, air filter restricting airflow, circuit breaker tripped, gas valve closed, or pilot light out on older furnaces.

DIY Solutions: Verify thermostat is set to heat and above room temperature. Replace air filter. Check circuit breaker (reset if tripped). Verify gas valve is open. If you have a standing pilot light (older furnaces), it may need relighting—consult your furnace manual for instructions. If it won't stay lit, call a professional.

When to Call a Pro: If none of these solutions work, your furnace likely has an electrical issue, ignition problem, or safety switch failure requiring a certified technician.

Problem 2: Furnace Blows Cold Air

Causes: Thermostat fan set to ON instead of AUTO, dirty flame sensor, or dirty air filter.

DIY Solutions: Check your thermostat—the fan setting should be AUTO, not ON. The ON setting runs the blower continuously even when the furnace isn't heating (feels like cold air). Switch it to AUTO. Replace your air filter if it's restricting airflow.

When to Call a Pro: If the fan is already on AUTO and you're still getting cold air, the flame sensor is likely dirty or the furnace isn't igniting properly. This requires professional cleaning or repair.

Problem 3: Furnace Short Cycles

Causes: Furnace overheating, dirty air filter, or thermostat in wrong location (near heat source).

DIY Solutions: Replace your air filter immediately—a clogged filter causes the furnace to overheat and shut down prematurely. Make sure your thermostat isn't positioned near a heating vent, sunny window, or heat-generating appliance that makes it sense false temperature.

When to Call a Pro: If short cycling persists after filter replacement, you may have a limit switch failure or other internal problem requiring professional diagnosis.

Problem 4: Uneven Heating From Room to Room

Causes: Blocked supply registers, dampers in ducts partially closed, dirty air filter, or thermostat location issue.

DIY Solutions: Check all supply registers and make sure none are blocked by furniture or closed dampers. Verify all rooms' dampers (if you have them) are set to allow heat flow. Replace air filter to ensure proper airflow distribution.

When to Call a Pro: If airflow seems balanced but heating is still uneven, you may have ductwork leakage or blockage requiring professional ductwork inspection.

Problem 5: Strange Noises from the Furnace

Banging Noise: Usually indicates ignition delay—the furnace takes too long to ignite after gas flows, then ignites explosively. Can damage the heat exchanger over time.

Squealing Noise: Indicates a worn blower motor bearing, failing capacitor, or loose belt. The bearing or belt will eventually fail, leaving you without heat.

Rattling Noise: Usually a loose panel, ductwork connection, or internal furnace component. While not immediately dangerous, it indicates maintenance neglect.

When to Call a Pro: Any unusual furnace noise warrants professional inspection. These issues worsen quickly and can cause major damage if ignored.

Problem 6: Higher Than Normal Heating Bills

Causes: Poor insulation, air leakage, dirty air filter, aging furnace, or broken thermostat.

DIY Solutions: Replace air filter—a clogged filter makes your furnace work harder and costs more to operate. Seal obvious air leaks around windows, doors, and electrical outlets. Verify your thermostat is working correctly by setting it 5 degrees higher and confirming the furnace responds.

When to Call a Pro: If your bill increased without explanation, call for a professional energy audit and heating system inspection. Your furnace may be approaching end of life or operating inefficiently.

DIY vs Professional Repair: Know the Limits

Homeowners CAN safely do: Replace air filters, check thermostat settings, verify circuit breaker position, and confirm vents aren't blocked. These simple steps solve 30-40% of common heating problems.

Only a certified technician should handle: Gas line connections and pressure testing, electrical diagnostics, flame sensor cleaning or replacement, heat exchanger inspection, refrigerant handling (heat pumps), and any internal furnace components. Gas and electrical work can be dangerous.

Emergency Situations: Call 911 or Air Express Immediately

Smell Gas: Leave Immediately and Call 911

A gas smell indicates a potential gas leak. Leave your home, go to a neighbor's, and call 911. Do not attempt to locate the leak. Once fire department clears your home, call Air Express for repair.

Carbon Monoxide Alarm Activated

Leave immediately. Carbon monoxide is deadly. Go to fresh air, call 911, then call Air Express. Carbon monoxide can come from a cracked heat exchanger or blocked venting.

No Heat When Temperature Below Freezing

This is a true emergency. Without heat, pipes can freeze, causing thousands of dollars in damage within hours. Call Air Express immediately at (801) 766-8585 for emergency heating repair.

Visible Flames or Smoke from Furnace

Turn off the furnace immediately and leave your home. Call 911. Do not attempt to operate the furnace. A fire hazard exists.

Utah-Specific Heating Considerations

Frozen Pipes Risk When Furnace Fails - Utah's harsh winters mean pipes can freeze within hours of furnace failure in uninsulated spaces. Frozen pipes burst and cause severe water damage. This makes furnace emergency repair critical.

Altitude Affects Pilot Light and Ignition - Utah's elevation affects gas combustion. Pilot lights and ignition systems adjusted for sea level may not work properly at Utah altitude. Professional adjustment ensures reliable winter performance.

Wind Chill on Exposed Outdoor Equipment - Utah's wind can create dangerous wind chills. Exposed outdoor equipment suffers stress in extreme cold. Make sure your furnace location and venting won't be blocked by snow or ice.
