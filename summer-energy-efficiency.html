Summer Energy Efficiency Guide: Keep Cooling Costs Down in Utah

Quick Answer: Utah Homeowners Can Cut Summer Cooling Costs 20-30%

Utah homeowners can significantly reduce summer cooling expenses by implementing multiple energy efficiency strategies. The most effective approach combines thermostat adjustments, home sealing, window coverings, and AC maintenance. Most Utah homeowners reduce cooling costs by 20-30% through a combination of these tactics. Better yet, many changes require no upfront investment and start saving money immediately.

Top 10 Summer Energy Efficiency Tips for Utah Homes

  1. Set Your Programmable Thermostat to 78°F When Home - The U.S. Department of Energy recommends 78°F for occupied spaces. Each degree above 78°F saves 1-3% on cooling costs. If comfortable, set 80°F during the day. Set higher temperatures (82-85°F) when away from home for 8+ hours.
  2. Seal Air Leaks Around Windows and Doors - Caulk or weatherstrip gaps that let cool air escape. Pay special attention to basement rim joists and attic penetrations. Professional energy audits identify major leakage areas costing little to seal.
  3. Add Insulation to Your Attic - Attics are the largest source of heat gain in summer. Adding insulation to R-30 or higher keeps heat out and cool air in. Utah's dry climate makes attic insulation particularly cost-effective.
  4. Use Ceiling Fans Strategically - Ceiling fans create air circulation that makes you feel cooler without lowering actual temperature. This allows you to set your thermostat higher while maintaining comfort.
  5. Close Blinds on South and West-Facing Windows - The sun's heat enters through windows, especially south and west sides. Closing blinds during peak heat hours (2-8 PM) prevents significant heat gain and reduces cooling load.
  6. Maintain Your AC Filter Monthly - Dirty filters reduce AC efficiency by forcing your compressor to work harder. Check your filter every month during cooling season and replace every 1-3 months depending on dust levels.
  7. Keep Your Outdoor Unit Clear and Clean - Debris around the condenser unit blocks airflow. Keep at least 2 feet clearance around all sides. Schedule a professional coil cleaning in spring before cooling season peaks.
  8. Use Bathroom and Kitchen Exhaust Fans Strategically - Running exhaust fans while cooking or showering removes heat and humidity quickly. Turn them off once the room cools. Don't run them longer than necessary or you'll exhaust conditioned air.
  9. Avoid Running Heat-Generating Appliances During Peak Hours - Run your dishwasher, laundry, and oven before 2 PM or after 8 PM when outside temperatures are lower. These appliances add significant heat to your home during peak afternoon hours.
  10. Consider a Whole-House Fan - In early morning or evening when outside temperatures drop below indoor temps, a whole-house fan can quickly cool your home using outdoor air instead of AC. This can reduce cooling costs 10-20% during shoulder seasons.

Understanding Your Summer Energy Bill: Peak vs Off-Peak Rates

Rocky Mountain Power, the primary utility serving Utah County and Salt Lake County, charges higher rates during peak demand hours. Understanding these rates helps you shift energy use to cheaper times. Rocky Mountain Power's typical summer schedule charges peak rates between 2 PM and 8 PM on weekdays. These peak-hour rates are often 50% higher than off-peak rates, making peak-hour consumption extremely expensive.

Smart Strategies: Pre-cool your home to 74-75°F before 2 PM, then raise the thermostat to 78-80°F during peak hours (2-8 PM). Your AC won't need to run much during peak rates because you started with a cool house. After 8 PM, cool back down to 78°F. Time-of-use smart thermostats automate this process. Check Rocky Mountain Power's website for your specific rate schedule—rates vary by location and plan type.

Signs Your AC Is Working Too Hard: When to Call a Professional

If your AC runs continuously without cooling your home adequately, or if your energy bills spike unexpectedly, your system needs professional inspection. Common signs of AC strain include:

  • AC running 24/7 but not reaching target temperature
  • Uneven cooling between rooms
  • Warm air blowing from vents
  • Higher than normal energy bills
  • AC making unusual noises or sounds
  • Visible frost on refrigerant lines

These issues often indicate low refrigerant, dirty coils, or system problems requiring professional repair. Air Express can diagnose and fix efficiency-sapping issues quickly.

Utah-Specific Energy Efficiency Considerations

Dry Heat Perfect for Evaporative Cooling - Utah's ultra-low humidity (often below 20%) makes evaporative cooling (swamp coolers) highly effective for supplementing AC. Evaporative coolers use 75% less energy than AC but work best when humidity is below 40%. Running your swamp cooler at night (when humidity is typically lowest) and using AC during hot afternoons provides optimal efficiency.

High Altitude Means Harder AC Work - Utah's elevation (4,000-11,000 feet) means thinner air and lower air density. Your AC compressor works harder at altitude to produce the same cooling effect. Proper AC maintenance and sizing become even more important. Systems oversized for sea level may be undersized for mountain elevations.

UV Exposure Damages Outdoor Units Faster - Utah's 300+ days of annual sunshine accelerate UV damage to outdoor AC units. The sun breaks down rubber seals, paint, and electrical insulation. Consider shade structures or UV-protective coatings for your outdoor unit to extend its lifespan and maintain efficiency.

Smart Thermostat ROI: Does It Really Pay for Itself?

Modern smart thermostats cost $150-$300 installed but typically save $10-$15 monthly on cooling costs (10-15% reduction). This means payback in 12-30 months, after which you pocket the savings. Smart thermostats offer additional benefits including remote control, usage reports, air filter reminders, and integration with smart home systems.

Look for ENERGY STAR-certified smart thermostats and models compatible with your HVAC system. Some utility companies offer rebates that further reduce costs. If you already have a basic programmable thermostat and adjust it seasonally, your savings may be smaller, but smart thermostats still typically pay for themselves within 2-3 years.
